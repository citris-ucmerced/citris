BioSCape

The BIOSCape program is NASA's first biodiversity campaign, aimed at understanding the diversity of life, the factors driving it, and predicting how it may change in the future. This is especially pertinent given the ongoing extinction crisis and the need to protect life on Earth. The University of California, Merced plays a key role in this project, led by Professor Erin Hestir. The campaign focuses on South Africa's Greater Cape Floristic Region (GCFR), a region known for its high biodiversity, including a wealth of unique marine animals. This region is similar in climate to California's Central Valley and faces similar pressures such as population growth, wildfires, water scarcity, and climate change. In collaboration with Professor Adam Wilson from the State University of New York at Buffalo and local partners in South Africa, the BIOSCape campaign will deploy state-of-the-art NASA technology. Two NASA aircraft will collect high-resolution images of the Cape and its coast, capturing data beyond the visible color spectrum, including ultraviolet and thermal wavelengths as well as LIDAR (a method for measuring distances by illuminating the target with laser light and measuring the reflection with a sensor). These data will enable researchers to create detailed maps of biodiversity, physiological traits of organisms, and the three-dimensional structure of ecosystems. Professor Hestir, apart from her leadership role in the project, also serves as Associate Director of the Center for Information Technology Research in the Interest of Society (CITRIS) and leads the EDGE in Tech Initiative: Expanding Diversity and Gender Equity at UC Merced. She plans to bring her work on the BIOSCape project into the classroom at UC Merced. The NASA Airborne Science Program supports the BIOSCape mission, providing the aircraft and sensors necessary for the data collection. The research projects and flight plans for the mission will be carried out by a Science Team, selected from the NASA Research Opportunities in Space and Earth Sciences (ROSES) applications.
